Missouri Wage Garnishment — RSMo 525.030, the Head-of-Family Cap, and the Automatic Stay

Missouri follows the federal garnishment framework but adds one of the stronger head-of-family protections in the country. Below is how ordinary consumer-debt garnishment works under Missouri law, how bankruptcy halts it, and when already-garnished wages can be clawed back.

Primary sources only. Every rule on this page is tied to a specific statute. Dollar amounts and percentages that depend on the federal minimum wage, § 547(c)(8) adjustments, or state-law amendments change periodically — always verify against the current statutory text before acting. Links to primary sources are in the Primary Sources appendix.

1. The Missouri Cap on Ordinary Garnishments — RSMo 525.030

For an ordinary consumer-debt garnishment (credit card, medical bill, personal loan reduced to judgment), the cap in RSMo 525.030.2 applies. The creditor may garnish the lesser of:

  1. 25% of disposable earnings for the pay period, or
  2. the amount by which disposable earnings exceed 30× the federal minimum hourly wage multiplied by the number of weeks in the pay period.

If neither is available (earnings below the floor), nothing is garnishable on that check.

Authority: RSMo 525.030.2; federal parallel at 15 U.S.C. § 1673(a).

Worked example — weekly pay, non-head-of-family

At a federal minimum wage of $7.25/hr, the 30×-multiplier floor per week is $217.50. If disposable earnings for a week are $800:

  • 25% of $800 = $200
  • $800 − $217.50 = $582.50
  • Lesser of the two = $200 garnishable

If disposable earnings for the same week were only $250:

  • 25% of $250 = $62.50
  • $250 − $217.50 = $32.50
  • Lesser of the two = $32.50 garnishable

Note: This worked example uses the long-standing $7.25/hr federal minimum. Missouri's state minimum is higher, but the garnishment formula in both 15 U.S.C. § 1673(a) and RSMo 525.030.2 is keyed to the federal figure. Verify current figures before relying.

2. The Head-of-Family Cap: 10% Instead of 25%

Missouri materially increases protection for a head of a family. Under RSMo 525.030.2, the 25% cap drops to 10% of disposable earnings for the head-of-family debtor. The 30×-federal-minimum-wage floor still applies.

How "head of family" is treated

The statute does not precisely define the phrase. Missouri practice generally treats it as a person providing material support for a spouse, child, or other dependent in the household. The reduction is not automatic — the debtor typically must raise the status, either in the answer to the garnishment (traverse) or by motion to the court of judgment. Supporting documentation helps: dependents on federal tax returns, household expense records, school enrollment, or evidence of court-ordered child support being paid.

Authority: RSMo 525.030.2; procedural posture governed by Chapter 525 RSMo (Attachment and Garnishment).

Do not assume the head-of-family cap applies without filing something. Missouri garnishment summonses do not calculate the 10% rate for the employer by default. If the debtor does not assert head-of-family status, the employer will typically withhold at 25%. Raising it after wages have already been taken is harder.

3. What "Disposable Earnings" Means

Under 15 U.S.C. § 1672(b) and tracked by RSMo 525.030.1, disposable earnings are what remains of the paycheck after subtracting amounts required by law to be withheld. That includes:

  • Federal income tax withholding
  • State income tax withholding
  • Social Security and Medicare (FICA)
  • Mandatory retirement contributions required by law (limited — voluntary 401(k) deferrals generally are not required-by-law withholdings)

Voluntary deductions (health insurance, voluntary retirement contributions, union dues in most cases, charitable payroll deductions) are not subtracted from gross earnings when computing disposable earnings for garnishment. Employers sometimes calculate this incorrectly; the debtor should verify against the employer's pay stub breakdown.

4. How Bankruptcy Stops a Garnishment

The moment a bankruptcy petition is filed under Chapter 7 or Chapter 13, the automatic stay of 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(6) stops any act to collect, assess, or recover a prepetition claim — which includes continued wage withholding on a prepetition consumer-debt garnishment.

What the debtor must do

  1. Notify the employer's payroll department immediately. Provide (a) the bankruptcy case number, (b) the exact date and time of filing, (c) the court (W.D. Mo. or E.D. Mo.), and (d) a copy of the Notice of Chapter 7/13 Case filed by the clerk, typically arriving a few days later.
  2. Notify the garnishing creditor's attorney the same day. Under § 362 the stay binds them regardless of notice, but practical compliance requires direct contact.
  3. Watch the next two pay cycles. If withholding continues after notice, the debtor has a stay-violation claim under 11 U.S.C. § 362(k) for individual debtors (actual damages, costs, and, where willful and egregious, punitive damages).

Exceptions that matter. 11 U.S.C. § 362(b) exempts certain acts from the stay. Most important in the wage-garnishment context:

  • § 362(b)(2)(C) — withholding of income that is property of the estate or earnings for payment of a domestic support obligation under a judicial or administrative order. Support garnishments continue.
  • § 362(b)(4) — police/regulatory actions by a governmental unit. Limited application to wage garnishment; most tax levies are not within (b)(4) and are stayed.

5. Recovering Wages Already Garnished — the 90-Day Preference Window

Garnished wages taken in the 90 days before the bankruptcy filing may be recoverable under 11 U.S.C. § 547(b) as a preferential transfer. The elements are:

  1. Transfer of the debtor's property (wages become debtor's property when earned).
  2. To or for the benefit of a creditor.
  3. On account of an antecedent debt.
  4. Made while the debtor was insolvent — presumed during the 90 days before filing, § 547(f).
  5. Made within 90 days before filing (one year for insiders).
  6. That lets the creditor recover more than it would in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation.

Routine consumer-debt garnishments on a judgment creditor usually satisfy all six elements. The trustee's avoidance power is primary; however, if the debtor has unused exemption room covering the recovered funds, the debtor can sometimes claim and keep the recovered wages under 11 U.S.C. § 522(g)/(h).

Important exceptions

  • § 547(c)(8) — in a consumer-debt case, transfers aggregating less than the statutory threshold (adjusted periodically under § 104) are excepted. Verify the current § 547(c)(8) threshold before assuming small garnishments are recoverable.
  • § 547(c)(7) — excepts bona-fide domestic-support-obligation payments.
  • Contemporaneous exchange / ordinary course exceptions (§ 547(c)(1), (c)(2)) rarely apply to garnishment but are sometimes argued.

Authority: 11 U.S.C. § 547(b), (c), (f); § 522(g), (h).

6. Domestic Support Obligations — Higher Caps, Different Treatment

Support orders (child support, spousal maintenance) follow different garnishment limits and are not stopped by the automatic stay.

SituationFederal cap (15 U.S.C. § 1673(b))
Debtor supports another spouse or child (not subject to the support order)50% of disposable earnings
Debtor supports another spouse or child and support is 12+ weeks in arrears55%
Debtor does not support another spouse or child60%
Above, plus 12+ weeks in arrears65%

Because § 362(b)(2)(C) exempts withholding for domestic-support orders, these garnishments continue through a bankruptcy. A domestic support obligation is generally nondischargeable under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(5).

Authority: 15 U.S.C. § 1673(b); 11 U.S.C. §§ 362(b)(2)(C), 523(a)(5), 507(a)(1).

7. IRS and Missouri Department of Revenue Tax Levies

Tax levies follow different mechanics. The IRS levies wages under 26 U.S.C. § 6331 with exempt amounts set by 26 U.S.C. § 6334 (typically a small weekly personal-exemption-plus-standard-deduction figure, far less than the RSMo 525.030 shield). Missouri DOR has parallel state authority.

Automatic stay still applies. Tax levies — unlike some common assumptions — are generally stopped by the 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(6) automatic stay on filing. They do not qualify for the § 362(b)(4) governmental-unit exception because levy collection is not a police/regulatory action.

But: income-tax debt is often nondischargeable. The three tests in 11 U.S.C. §§ 523(a)(1) and 507(a)(8) (three-year return-due date, two-year return-filed date, 240-day assessment) must be met for dischargeability of older income tax. Most recent tax debt survives bankruptcy. See Missouri Debt Collection Law for how the stay interacts with other post-judgment collection.

8. Federal Student Loan Administrative Garnishment

The U.S. Department of Education and its guaranty agencies can administratively garnish up to 15% of disposable earnings under 20 U.S.C. § 1095a — without a court judgment. No head-of-family reduction applies; the 30×-minimum-wage floor does.

Filing bankruptcy stops administrative wage garnishment the same way it stops a judgment garnishment — the § 362(a)(6) stay applies. But federal student loans are presumed nondischargeable absent an adversary proceeding proving undue hardship under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8). The Brunner test (widely adopted; the Eighth Circuit has its own Long totality-of-circumstances test) governs.

Authority: 20 U.S.C. § 1095a; 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8); In re Long, 322 F.3d 549 (8th Cir. 2003).

9. Multiple Garnishments at the Same Time

When more than one creditor has a garnishment against the same wages, Missouri law generally gives priority by service date — first served, first paid — subject to the aggregate cap. The total withheld for non-support garnishments cannot exceed the RSMo 525.030 cap (25% or 10% depending on head-of-family status). Support orders stack on top of the ordinary cap up to the § 1673(b) ceilings shown above.

A subsequent creditor's garnishment is typically held in abeyance until the earlier one is satisfied — meaning a second judgment creditor may wait months or years to reach the debtor's wages while the first garnishment runs its course. Bankruptcy resolves all of them at once.

10. How to Stop a Missouri Garnishment Without Bankruptcy — the Traverse

The traverse is Missouri's statutory vehicle for contesting a garnishment. Under Chapter 525 RSMo, a debtor (or employer, as garnishee) may answer the garnishment summons and raise defenses including:

  • Head-of-family status under RSMo 525.030.2
  • Exempt funds (Social Security, SSI, veterans' benefits, public assistance — typically not wages but sometimes relevant to bank-account garnishments)
  • Wage amount below the 30×-federal-minimum-wage floor
  • The underlying judgment is void (lack of service, expired, satisfied)

File the traverse in the court that issued the judgment. Deadlines are tight and vary by division. If the debtor's primary issue is overall debt load, the traverse is a temporary measure — it does not discharge the underlying judgment, which remains collectible against future wages until the judgment expires (generally 10 years in Missouri, renewable; see Missouri SOL on judgments, RSMo 516.350).

FAQ

Is wage garnishment public record?

The garnishment filing (writ, notice, answer, return) is part of the court file of the underlying judgment and is public record. Employers see it because they are served as garnishee. Co-workers typically do not unless payroll handling is unusually indiscreet. In some states there is explicit anti-discrimination protection for an employee with a single garnishment (15 U.S.C. § 1674(a)) — termination for a first garnishment is prohibited; Missouri follows the federal rule.

Can my employer fire me for having a wage garnishment?

Federal law (15 U.S.C. § 1674(a)) prohibits terminating an employee because of garnishment for one indebtedness. A second or subsequent garnishment for a different debt removes that shield, but many employers choose not to terminate even then. The Wage and Hour Division of the U.S. Department of Labor enforces § 1674 violations.

How long does a Missouri judgment stay collectible against my wages?

A Missouri judgment is generally collectible for ten years from the date of entry, then renewable once under RSMo 516.350 for another ten years if the creditor revives before expiration — effectively up to twenty years. Each renewal attempt must be made before the judgment lapses. See the SOL section of the Debt Collection Law page for the full treatment, including the Jackson County revival dynamics.

If I file Chapter 7 and the garnishment is stopped, what happens to the creditor's judgment?

The underlying judgment survives filing unless it is a personal liability discharged by the bankruptcy. In a typical consumer Chapter 7, the debtor's personal liability on the underlying debt is discharged at the end of the case (usually ~3–4 months after filing), which extinguishes the judgment as a collection tool against the debtor personally. The judgment remains as an in-rem lien on any non-exempt real property unless avoided under 11 U.S.C. § 522(f) (for judicial liens impairing an exemption).

Does Missouri's 10% head-of-family cap apply to a bank account levy (not wages)?

No. The 25% / 10% / 30×-minimum-wage rules in RSMo 525.030 apply to wages being garnished while still held by the employer. Once wages have been paid into a bank account, the funds in the account are subject to bank-account garnishment rules, which are narrower protections — typically limited to Social Security, SSI, veterans' benefits, or state public-assistance tracing. Filing bankruptcy stops a bank levy the same way it stops a wage garnishment (§ 362(a)(6) stay).
