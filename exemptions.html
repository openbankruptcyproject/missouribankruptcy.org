Missouri Bankruptcy Exemptions

If you file bankruptcy in Missouri, the property you get to keep is defined by Missouri state law, not federal bankruptcy exemptions. That single rule shapes every other decision about what you keep, what you lose, and whether bankruptcy is even the right move.

Missouri Opted Out of the Federal Exemption Scheme

Congress gave every state the option to let its residents choose between federal bankruptcy exemptions (listed in 11 U.S.C. § 522(d)) or the state's own exemption scheme. Missouri chose to opt out. Under RSMo § 513.427 (effective August 28, 1982), a Missouri resident filing bankruptcy must use the Missouri exemption scheme. The federal § 522(d) list is off the table.

Practically, that means the dollar amounts below are the ceiling on what you can protect. They are smaller than the federal amounts in almost every category. Missouri's homestead exemption, in particular, is far lower than the federal figure. Plan accordingly.

Homestead Exemption - Up to $15,000

Under RSMo § 513.475 (effective August 28, 2003), your principal residence - a dwelling house with its appurtenances and the land it sits on - is exempt up to $15,000 in equity. The exemption extends to rents, issues, and products of the homestead.

Common mistake: Married couples do NOT get a combined $30,000. If you and your spouse both own the home, you share one $15,000 homestead between you. Missouri does not stack individual homesteads on a single property - the combined exemption of all owners of the same homestead cannot exceed $15,000. This cap is in the statute itself (RSMo § 513.475) and has been applied consistently by Eighth Circuit BAP case law.

One Missouri-specific wrinkle: neither spouse can unilaterally sell, mortgage, or transfer the homestead. The statute voids any such action. To convey or encumber the homestead, both spouses must act jointly. This protects the non-filing spouse in some scenarios but constrains strategic options.

Motor Vehicle - Up to $3,000

Under RSMo § 513.430.1(5) (effective August 28, 2022), you can protect up to $3,000 in aggregate value across motor vehicles. If your vehicle is worth less than $3,000 over any liens, it is fully exempt. If it is worth more, the trustee can sell it and pay you $3,000 before distributing the rest to creditors - though in practice, most Missouri Chapter 7 trustees will abandon vehicles that would yield minimal value after sale costs.

Household Goods and Personal Property - Up to $3,000

RSMo § 513.430.1(1) exempts household furnishings, household goods, wearing apparel, appliances, books, animals, crops, and musical instruments for personal or family use, up to $3,000 aggregate. For most Missouri filers this covers the contents of a home comfortably; only unusually-valuable collections (antiques, high-end electronics, valuable instruments) press the cap.

Tools of the Trade - Up to $3,000

RSMo § 513.430.1(4) protects implements, books, and tools of trade up to $3,000 aggregate. A contractor's tools, a mechanic's equipment, a nurse's scope - this is the exemption that keeps you working after bankruptcy.

Wedding Ring, Jewelry, and Firearms

RSMo § 513.430.1(2) protects a wedding ring up to $1,500 plus other jewelry up to $500 aggregate. The wedding ring is separately protected at the higher figure; all other jewelry combines into the $500 pool.

Missouri separately exempts firearms, firearms accessories, and ammunition up to $1,500 aggregate under RSMo § 513.430.1(12). Missouri added this category relatively recently; most other states lump firearms into general personal-property exemptions.

Wildcard Stacking - $600 Plus Head-of-Family

Missouri offers two stackable "wildcard" exemptions - property the debtor can choose from any category:

  1. General wildcard ($600): RSMo § 513.430.1(3) lets any debtor apply up to $600 aggregate against any property the statute does not otherwise exempt.
  2. Head-of-family wildcard ($1,250 + $350 per dependent): Under RSMo § 513.440 (effective August 28, 2012), the head of a family who is a Missouri resident can exempt an additional $1,250 plus $350 per unmarried dependent child under 21 (or SSA-determined disabled dependent). Exception: 10% of debts, income, salary, or wages due to the head of family are NOT exempt.

For a married filer with two minor children, stacking the general wildcard plus head-of-family gives an additional $1,850 + $700 = $2,550 of flexibility on top of the category-specific exemptions.

Wage Exemption - 75% or Better Protected

RSMo § 525.030 (effective January 2, 1979) caps garnishment at the lesser of:

  • 25% of aggregate weekly earnings after legally required deductions;
  • The amount by which weekly earnings exceed 30 times the federal minimum hourly wage;
  • 10% if the debtor is a head of family and a Missouri resident.

Court-ordered family support, bankruptcy proceedings, and tax debts bypass these restrictions. For most Missouri workers, at minimum 75% of wages are shielded from non-support garnishment - and heads of family get 90% protection. More on wage garnishment defense.

Retirement Accounts - Broadly Protected

RSMo § 513.430.1(10) exempts qualified retirement plans under Internal Revenue Code §§ 401(a), 403(a), 403(b), 408 (IRAs), 408A (Roth IRAs), and 409. Social Security, unemployment, veterans', and disability benefits are separately exempt, along with alimony up to $750 per month.

Federal ERISA preemption also protects most employer-sponsored retirement accounts regardless of state exemption law.

Life Insurance

RSMo § 513.430.1(7) exempts unmatured life insurance contracts and funeral/burial proceeds up to $15,000. Accrued cash value, dividends, and loan value are separately exempt under § 513.430.1(8) with a $150,000 cap in bankruptcy - but contracts purchased within one year before the bankruptcy filing are not exempt (a look-back meant to prevent last-minute asset conversion).

Tenancy by the Entirety - Missouri's Biggest Strategic Lever

Missouri recognizes tenancy by the entirety for married couples holding real property jointly. Under established Missouri case law - confirmed by the Eighth Circuit - property held as tenants by the entirety is exempt from claims against only one spouse, as long as the debt is individual and not joint.

Eighth Circuit doctrine: The Eighth Circuit has recognized that when a creditor holds a judgment against only one spouse and the underlying debt is individual (not joint), a judicial lien against Missouri entireties property may be avoidable as an impairment of the homestead exemption in the non-debtor spouse's interest. Missouri treats entireties as a single undividable interest, which is the doctrinal foundation. Fact patterns vary significantly - specific case law application should be reviewed with counsel before relying on this protection in any specific case.

Practical impact: if only one spouse files bankruptcy, the non-filing spouse's share in entireties property is generally protected from the filing spouse's individual creditors. This can make the difference between keeping and losing a home that exceeds the $15,000 homestead cap.

This is a complex area with fact-specific application. A Missouri debtor with significant equity in entireties property should not file pro se without understanding the entireties analysis specific to their facts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Missouri allow federal bankruptcy exemptions?

No. Missouri opted out of the federal exemption scheme in 1982 under RSMo § 513.427. Missouri residents filing bankruptcy must use Missouri state exemptions and cannot claim the federal exemptions in 11 U.S.C. § 522(d).

What is Missouri's homestead exemption amount?

Under RSMo § 513.475, Missouri protects up to $15,000 of equity in the dwelling house that serves as your homestead, along with associated land. The statute itself caps the combined exemption of all owners of the same homestead at $15,000 - joint owners cannot each claim $15,000 separately.

Can married Missouri filers double their homestead exemption?

No. When both spouses own the same homestead, RSMo 513.475 caps the combined exemption at $15,000 - they share one homestead exemption, not two. This is different from federal practice and a common source of planning errors.

How does the Missouri head-of-family wildcard work?

RSMo § 513.440 provides a head-of-family wildcard of $1,250 base plus $350 for each unmarried dependent child under 21 (or SSA-disabled dependent). This stacks with the general $600 wildcard in RSMo § 513.430.1(3). Exception: 10% of debts, income, salary, or wages due to the head of family are not exempt.

What is tenancy by the entirety and why does it matter in Missouri bankruptcy?

Missouri recognizes tenancy by the entirety for married couples holding real property jointly. Property held this way is generally treated as exempt from claims against only one spouse when the debt is individual and not joint. This is one of the most important protections for Missouri married filers with non-joint debts, but the analysis is fact-specific - consult counsel for significant entireties property.

How does Missouri wage garnishment interact with bankruptcy?

RSMo § 525.030 caps non-support wage garnishment at the lesser of 25% of disposable weekly earnings, the amount exceeding 30 times the federal minimum wage, or 10% if the debtor is a head of family and Missouri resident. That 10% cap for heads of family - 90% of wages protected - is one of the strongest state-level wage protections in the country. Bankruptcy's automatic stay halts garnishment immediately on filing; for non-bankruptcy wage defense, see stopwagegarnishment.com.
