Missouri Bankruptcy Case Law - Decisions Worth Knowing

Missouri bankruptcy cases are governed by federal bankruptcy law filtered through Missouri state law on exemptions, property rights, and debt enforcement. Below are Eighth Circuit and 8th Cir. BAP decisions with direct bearing on Missouri filers.

Topics Where Eighth Circuit Case Law Shapes Missouri Practice

Rather than list specific case citations without having opened each opinion ourselves, this page identifies the Missouri-specific doctrines where 8th Circuit and 8th Cir. BAP case law is controlling. When researching any of these, pull the governing case directly from CourtListener or the 8th Circuit opinion index before relying on any summary - including ours.

Tenancy by the Entirety - Lien Avoidance When Only One Spouse Is the Debtor

Missouri treats property held as tenants by the entirety (TBE) as a single undivided interest owned by the marital unit. Eighth Circuit case law addresses when a judicial lien against one spouse may be avoidable in that spouse's Chapter 7 bankruptcy where the underlying judgment is not joint. This is one of the most important planning doctrines for Missouri married filers with significant non-joint debts. Application is fact-specific - titling, timing of lien, and joint-vs-separate debt status all matter. Pull the controlling case on CourtListener before relying on it; see the exemptions page for the doctrinal overview.

Missouri Homestead - Statutory Cap on Joint Owners

The Missouri homestead exemption (RSMo § 513.475) caps the combined exemption of all owners of the same homestead at $15,000 - joint owners cannot each claim $15,000 separately. Eighth Circuit BAP case law has applied this cap consistently. Combined with Missouri's opt-out from the federal exemption scheme (RSMo § 513.427), this makes Missouri's homestead materially weaker than the federal exemption scheme (11 U.S.C. § 522(d)(1)) would have offered.

Judicial Lien Avoidance Under 11 U.S.C. § 522(f)

Lien avoidance under § 522(f) is one of the most fact-intensive areas of Missouri bankruptcy practice. Whether a specific judicial lien impairs a specific exemption depends on equity calculation, lien timing, and the interaction with Missouri's exemption scheme. Research the specific lien type and exemption claim on CourtListener before concluding a lien is avoidable.

Subchapter V in the Eighth Circuit

Subchapter V of Chapter 11 - the small-business reorganization tool created by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 - has grown substantially in the Eighth Circuit since 2020. Sub V case law is still developing in both Missouri bankruptcy districts. See the filing-practice page for the 60-day status-conference (11 U.S.C. § 1188) and 90-day plan-filing (11 U.S.C. § 1189) timelines.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can married Missouri filers double the homestead exemption?

No. Under RSMo § 513.475, joint owners of the same homestead cannot each claim a $15,000 exemption on top of each other - the statute caps the total exemption per homestead at $15,000 regardless of how many owners claim it. This is a common planning error and makes Missouri's homestead materially weaker than federal exemptions, which Missouri opted out of in 1982.

What topics does Eighth Circuit case law affect for Missouri filers?

Missouri-specific doctrines where 8th Cir. and 8th Cir. BAP case law is controlling include: tenancy-by-the-entirety treatment when only one spouse is the debtor, the joint-owner cap in the Missouri homestead exemption, judicial lien avoidance under 11 U.S.C. § 522(f), Missouri opt-out from the federal exemption scheme, and the application of Missouri's statute of limitations and partial-payment revival rules to scheduled debts. Specific case citations on each topic should be pulled directly from CourtListener or the 8th Cir. opinion index before being relied on.
