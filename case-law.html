Missouri Bankruptcy Case Law — Decisions Worth Knowing

Missouri bankruptcy cases are governed by federal bankruptcy law filtered through Missouri state law on exemptions, property rights, and debt enforcement. Below are Eighth Circuit and 8th Cir. BAP decisions with direct bearing on Missouri filers.

Tenancy by the Entirety — Judicial Liens Against One Spouse Are Avoidable

CRP Holdings A-1 LLC v. O'Sullivan (In re O'Sullivan)

No. 17-3226 (8th Cir. Feb. 1, 2019) · Opinion PDF (8th Cir.)

The Eighth Circuit held that a judicial lien on Missouri real property held as tenants by the entirety — where the underlying judgment was against only the husband, not the wife — was avoidable in the husband's Chapter 7 bankruptcy as an impairment of the homestead exemption.

The creditor's approximately $800,000 judgment had attached to the husband's interest only, but because Missouri treats the entireties as a single undividable interest, the lien impaired the couple's full homestead claim.

Practical meaning. If your spouse is not a co-debtor on a judgment and your home is titled as tenants by the entirety, the judicial lien can generally be avoided in bankruptcy. This is one of the most important single doctrines for Missouri married debtors. Application is fact-specific (titling, timing of lien, joint-vs-separate debt status). See the exemptions page for more.

Missouri Homestead Exemption — Joint Owners Cannot Double Up

In re Hurd

441 B.R. 116 (8th Cir. BAP 2010)

The Eighth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel held that under RSMo § 513.475, joint owners of the same homestead cannot each claim a $15,000 exemption on top of each other. The statute caps the total exemption per homestead at $15,000 regardless of how many owners claim it.

Practical meaning. Married filers hoping to stack $30,000 on the same home will not succeed. Combined with Missouri's opt-out from the federal exemption scheme (RSMo § 513.427), this makes Missouri's homestead protection materially weaker than the federal exemption scheme (11 U.S.C. § 522(d)(1)) would have offered.

Subchapter V in the Eighth Circuit — Rapid Recent Growth

Subchapter V of Chapter 11 — the small-business reorganization tool created by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 — has seen sharp recent growth in the Eighth Circuit. Filings in the first four months of 2024 rose approximately 275% over the same period in 2023.

Source: Thompson Coburn LLP, "Subchapter V Practice in the Eighth Circuit: The First Four Years" (May 2024)

Frequently Asked Questions

What does In re O'Sullivan mean for Missouri married filers?

In CRP Holdings A-1 LLC v. O'Sullivan (In re O'Sullivan), No. 17-3226 (8th Cir. Feb. 1, 2019), the Eighth Circuit held that a judicial lien on Missouri real property held as tenants by the entirety — where the underlying judgment was against only one spouse — was avoidable in that spouse's Chapter 7 bankruptcy as an impairment of the homestead exemption. Practically, if only one spouse is a judgment debtor and the home is titled as tenants by the entirety, the judicial lien can generally be avoided in bankruptcy. Application is fact-specific.

Can married Missouri filers double the homestead exemption?

No. In In re Hurd, 441 B.R. 116 (8th Cir. BAP 2010), the Eighth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel held that under RSMo § 513.475, joint owners of the same homestead cannot each claim a $15,000 exemption on top of each other. The statute caps the total exemption per homestead at $15,000. This is a common planning error and makes Missouri's homestead materially weaker than federal exemptions, which Missouri opted out of in 1982.
