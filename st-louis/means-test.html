How the St. Louis Means Test Qualifies You for Chapter 7

The bankruptcy means test is federal, but the numbers that make or break your case are Missouri-specific. For St. Louis residents filing in the Eastern District of Missouri, the test begins by comparing your annualized six-month household income against the Missouri state median for your household size. Most filers pass here without ever reaching the expense-deduction calculation. Below, we walk through exactly how it works and what to do if you are close to the line.

The six-month lookback is strict: it ends on the last day of the calendar month before you file. If your income dropped sharply in the last month or two, consider whether waiting to file makes the lookback window friendlier. Social Security benefits are excluded from the calculation, and so are VA disability benefits, payments to victims of war crimes, and certain reparations. Everything else - wages, overtime, bonuses, tips, rental income, unemployment, business income, spouse income, and regular contributions from anyone who helps pay your bills - is counted as "current monthly income."

If your annualized CMI is under the Missouri median, you pass automatically. You still fill out Form 122A-1, but the means test is done and you can proceed with the rest of your Chapter 7 schedules. If you are above median, you move to Form 122A-2, where you subtract the IRS National Standards (food, clothing, personal care, out-of-pocket health), the Local Standards specific to St. Louis County and the City of St. Louis (housing, utilities, transportation), plus actual expenses for health insurance, taxes, childcare, court-ordered support, and the secured debt payments coming due in the next 60 months. What is left is your monthly disposable income. If that is below the statutory threshold, you pass.

St. Louis Filing Tips

  • Document Social Security separately. Trustees review Form 122A-1 Line 14 carefully. Exclude Social Security cleanly so the trustee does not challenge your math.
  • Use the correct household size. Include everyone who lives in your home and shares expenses, even adult children and unrelated dependents.
  • Watch the tax refund issue. A tax refund received within the six-month lookback does not count as income - it is a return of your own money - but cash on hand from the refund on filing day is an asset.
  • Keep your pay stubs. You will need to attach pay advices for the 60-day period before filing under 11 U.S.C. section 521(a)(1)(B)(iv).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Missouri median income for means test purposes?

The U.S. Trustee updates median income figures every six months or so. Check the current Missouri figures at justice.gov/ust/means-testing before you file. For most filers, the one-person median is in the low-to-mid $50K range and the four-person median is around $95K - but always verify the current number.

Do I really need to pass the means test to file Chapter 13 in St. Louis?

No. Chapter 13 does not have the same means test gate as Chapter 7. Chapter 13 has its own disposable income calculation on Form 122C that determines how much you pay into your plan, but there is no 'qualify or be dismissed' mechanic like Chapter 7.

What if I am self-employed and my income varies month to month?

Self-employed debtors report gross receipts minus ordinary and necessary operating expenses on Form 122A-1 Line 5. You should attach a profit and loss statement for the six-month lookback. Do not net out personal draws - those are how you pay yourself, not a business expense.
